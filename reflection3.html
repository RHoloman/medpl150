Reflection

For the sake of time, I stuck to the same format of candid photography to try out depth of field. While I understood the concept of the functions, it was pretty difficult to get the right settings for the shots I was trying to make, whether it be how I set up the camera or if there were any limitations with the camera (I tend to believe it was more so the former). As a result, some of my photos came out more blurry than I intended. Luckily, I did have a few shots that worked as intended, with one I used not only to test the camera raw filter, but to do the artificial blur as well.

Artificially adding the field blur to the image was also a bit tedious as I had to manually select the areas I wanted to blur. I did this by using the path tool to block and the shape of the area, then I converted that shape into a selection. It had to do that a couple of times since Some edges were blurred at all, making the artificial blur more noticeable. In the end, I agree with Professor Oh’s sentiment as artificial blur adds this unnatural feeling to the image, giving this smudged effect (most likely because I overdid it).

Overall, while tedious, it was good practice to manually get a shallow depth of field from the camera itself, and I can see myself translating what I learned from path selection into my digital art making process. If you’re reading this, the coding was a success…
